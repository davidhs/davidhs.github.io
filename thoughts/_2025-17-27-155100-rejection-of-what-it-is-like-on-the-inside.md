# Rejection of "What it is like on the inside"-view

When it comes to _subjective experience_, you ought to reject the notation that _there is something that is like to be something_.

You may have heard this from Thomas Nagel's essay "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?"

There is something I find deeply unsatisfying about this notion.  Why would anything have a "what-it-is-on-the-inside" subjective point of view?  It feels as if that is just given (and poorly justified).

Also, I am personally highly skeptical about assuming consciousness is fundamental to the physical universe or an axiom.  This is a _huge_ **if**.  If you like physicalism and you want consciousness to be fundamental, you've got yourself panpsychism. I think you ought to pick some lower hanging fruit before taking that assumption.

There are phenomena in consciousness where it appears we can give pretty good descriptions for, e.g. structural shapes, certain relationship between colors (e.g. different hues, brightness, etc.).  There is also a whole host of experiments which indicates all kinds of patterns that govern visual experience.  Stephen Gross'bergs magnus opus "Conscious Mind, Resonand Brain: How Each Brain Makes a Mind" is very well worth the read.

There are other phenomena of consciousness that seem much more opaque or impossible to "unravel" from the perspective of one's subjective experience.  For example, to me at least, color seems very information rich, yet I can not deconstruct color or talk about color in my experience as I can talk about shapes using mathematics.  The same goes for the fundamentals or primitives that relate to sound.

What is interesting though, through meditation you can in some sense deconstruct the experience of color, in the sense that parts of the experience of colors just disappears.  It doesn't reveal any internal structure to colors but you can put it offline.

It is interesting go to the website [Cheetah House](https://www.cheetahhouse.org/) and see how people have adversely affected their perception, including where the connection between the color red of a traffic light and the meaning "to stop" when you are driving is severed.

So, if you should reject "what it is like on the inside", what should you do instead?  You should try to find explicit structures that correspond to experience, e.g. mathematical structures or mathematical processes.  You can do some of it "on the inside", i.e. from your conscious subjective experience, but you can also examine brains "from the outside" to map these things together.

I am personally on the opinion if one applies "mechanistic interpretability" successfully to the brain, and is able to find how it correlates with experience, I think one would find that all of one's conscious experience and the relationships there within, are physically represented in the brain, exactly like how a program or software process has all its representative parts implemented in the physical computation hardware (e.g. as the evolving configuration of the electromagnetic field inside the circuitry of the machine).
